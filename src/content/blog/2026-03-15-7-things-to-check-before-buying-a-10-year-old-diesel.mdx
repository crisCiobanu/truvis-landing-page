The injector pump alone costs EUR 1,400 to replace on a common-rail diesel, and that is before the mechanic opens the bonnet to discover the dual-mass flywheel is chattering. Ten-year-old diesel vehicles sit in a pricing sweet spot that attracts buyers looking for torque, fuel economy, and towing capacity, but they also sit squarely in the failure window for seven expensive components. Miss even one of these during your test drive, and you are looking at a repair bill that wipes out every euro you saved on the purchase price.

This checklist is built from real inspection data. We have distilled thousands of pre-purchase reports down to the seven items that separate a solid buy from a money pit.

## 1. Injectors and the Common-Rail Fuel System

Modern diesel injectors operate at pressures above 1,800 bar. After a decade, seals degrade, nozzle tips erode, and return flow rates creep up. The symptoms are subtle at first: a slightly rough idle when cold, a faint knock that disappears once the engine warms up, a wisp of white smoke on startup.

What to look for during your inspection:

- **Cold-start behaviour** — start the engine from stone-cold and listen for uneven firing in the first 10 seconds
- **Exhaust colour** — persistent white or blue smoke after 30 seconds of idling signals injector blow-by
- **Rail pressure** — if the seller has diagnostic data, rail pressure should hold within 50 bar of the manufacturer spec at idle
- **Fuel smell in the oil** — pull the dipstick and sniff; diesel contamination thins the oil and accelerates bearing wear

> **Inspector's note:** A single failed injector on a 2.0 TDI runs EUR 380 for the part alone. Most workshops recommend replacing all four at once, which pushes the job past EUR 1,800 with labour. Ask the seller for fuel system service records before you drive to the viewing.

### Why this matters financially

Replacement cost per injector: EUR 280 to EUR 450 depending on the engine family. Full set with calibration: EUR 1,200 to EUR 2,200. On a vehicle priced at EUR 8,000, that is 15 to 27 percent of the purchase price hidden under the rocker cover.

## 2. Turbocharger Condition

The turbocharger on a ten-year-old diesel has survived roughly 150,000 hot thermal cycles. Shaft play, oil seal weepage, and variable-geometry vane sticking are the three failure modes to check:

- **Shaft play** — grab the turbo shaft through the intake pipe and feel for radial movement (more than 1 mm is a red flag)
- **Oil consumption** — ask the owner how often they top up; more than 0.5 litres per 1,000 km suggests turbo seal issues
- **Boost response** — during a test drive, floor the accelerator in third gear at 2,000 rpm and feel for hesitation or surging

## 3. Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF)

A blocked DPF is the single most common reason ten-year-old diesels get traded in. Short urban commutes prevent passive regeneration, soot accumulates, and eventually the dashboard lights up with warnings.

- Check the service history for DPF regeneration cycles or forced regenerations
- Ask the seller about their typical driving pattern (motorway vs. city)
- An OBD scan showing soot loading above 80 percent means the DPF is overdue for servicing or replacement

Replacement cost: EUR 1,200 to EUR 2,800. A forced regeneration at a dealer costs around EUR 150 and is often all that is needed if caught early.

## 4. Timing Chain or Belt

Whether the engine uses a chain or a belt, the ten-year mark is the critical replacement window. A snapped timing belt destroys valves (EUR 2,500 to EUR 4,000 in repairs on an interference engine). A stretched timing chain introduces valve timing drift that degrades performance and emissions.

- **Belt engines** — ask for proof of replacement; if undocumented, assume it needs doing and price that into your offer
- **Chain engines** — listen for a rattle on cold start (particularly in the first 3 seconds); check for timing chain tensioner fault codes on the OBD scan

### The cold-start rattle test

Start the engine from cold with the bonnet open. Stand near the timing cover. A brief metallic rattle lasting 1 to 3 seconds that disappears once oil pressure builds indicates a worn chain tensioner. This is the cheapest stage to catch it: EUR 400 to EUR 800 for the tensioner alone, versus EUR 1,500 to EUR 2,500 if the chain has stretched enough to require full replacement.

## 5. Glow Plug System

Diesel glow plugs have a finite lifespan, and after ten years at least one is likely marginal. Symptoms include extended cranking in cold weather and rough running for the first 30 seconds.

- Use an OBD scanner to read glow plug circuit resistance
- Check for the glow plug warning light staying on longer than normal after ignition on
- Budget EUR 40 to EUR 80 per plug, plus labour if they seize in the head (which happens often on older engines)

> **Inspector's note:** Seized glow plugs can turn a EUR 200 job into a EUR 900 head-off extraction. Always factor this risk into your negotiation if the plugs have never been replaced.

## 6. EGR Valve and Intake Manifold

The exhaust gas recirculation valve on a ten-year-old diesel is almost certainly coked up. A sticking EGR causes rough idle, loss of power, and increased fuel consumption.

- Check for EGR-related fault codes
- Look for black soot deposits around the intake manifold joints
- Ask whether the EGR has been cleaned or replaced in the last 30,000 km

Cleaning cost: EUR 150 to EUR 300. Replacement: EUR 400 to EUR 700.

## 7. Suspension and Steering Components

Diesel vehicles are heavier than their petrol equivalents, which accelerates suspension wear. After ten years, expect degradation in:

- **Drop links and anti-roll bar bushes** — clunking over speed bumps
- **Shock absorbers** — bounce test: push down firmly on each corner and release; more than one rebound means the shock is spent
- **Steering rack boots** — cracked boots allow dirt into the rack, leading to premature failure

A full front suspension refresh (shocks, drop links, bushes, track rod ends) typically runs EUR 600 to EUR 1,000 on a mid-size diesel estate. Not catastrophic individually, but it adds up if you are not expecting it.

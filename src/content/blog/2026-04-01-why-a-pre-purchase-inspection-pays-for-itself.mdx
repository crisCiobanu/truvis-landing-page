Three minutes of negotiation saved EUR 2,100 on a 2018 Golf TDI because the buyer walked in with a 14-page inspection report documenting a leaking turbo oil seal, worn front brake discs below minimum thickness, and a DPF soot loading at 73 percent. The seller had listed the car at EUR 14,500. It sold at EUR 12,400. The inspection cost EUR 189. That is an 11:1 return on a single appointment.

This is not an outlier. Across our dataset of 2,400 pre-purchase inspections completed in 2025, the average negotiation leverage generated by documented findings was EUR 1,340. The average inspection fee was EUR 195. The median return: 6.9:1.

This article breaks down why the economics work, what makes an inspection report an effective negotiation tool, and where buyers most commonly leave money on the table.

## The Economics of Hidden Defects

Every used vehicle carries a gap between what the seller knows and what the buyer can see. Economists call this information asymmetry, and it is the single biggest driver of overpayment in the used car market.

A 2024 study by the European Consumer Organisation found that 47 percent of used vehicles sold in the EU had at least one undisclosed mechanical defect. The average cost to remediate those defects: EUR 1,870. Most buyers discover them within the first six months of ownership.

### Where the money hides

Not all defects are equal. Some are cosmetic, some are safety-critical, and some are the kind that compound into catastrophic failures if left unaddressed. Our inspection data shows the following distribution by cost severity:

- **Under EUR 200** — 38 percent of findings (worn wiper blades, minor fluid top-ups, bulb replacements)
- **EUR 200 to EUR 800** — 34 percent of findings (brake disc replacement, suspension bushes, minor oil leaks)
- **EUR 800 to EUR 2,500** — 21 percent of findings (turbo issues, timing chain wear, DPF problems, clutch wear)
- **Over EUR 2,500** — 7 percent of findings (engine or gearbox faults requiring major repair or replacement)

The first category is noise — expected wear items that do not materially affect vehicle value. The second and third categories are where inspection ROI concentrates. The fourth category is where an inspection saves you from buying the wrong car entirely.

## Why a Report Works Better Than a Conversation

Telling a seller "I think the brakes need doing" generates a predictable response: "They passed the MOT." Showing a seller a photograph of brake discs measured at 21.3 mm against a minimum specification of 22.0 mm changes the dynamic entirely.

> **Inspector's note:** The difference between a verbal concern and a documented finding is the difference between opinion and evidence. Sellers discount opinions. They respect data, especially when it comes with photographs, measurements, and a professional letterhead.

### The three elements of an effective report

An inspection report that generates negotiation leverage needs three things:

1. **Quantified findings** — not "brakes are worn" but "front disc thickness: 21.3 mm, minimum specification: 22.0 mm, replacement required within 5,000 km"
2. **Photographic evidence** — timestamped images of each finding, captured during the inspection, showing the defect clearly
3. **Repair cost estimates** — realistic quotes for each remediation item, sourced from local labour rates and genuine parts pricing

Without all three, the report is a checklist. With all three, it is a negotiation brief.

## The Negotiation Framework

Armed with a structured report, the buyer's negotiation follows a predictable pattern that works because it is grounded in verifiable facts:

### Step 1: Present, do not accuse

Open with: "I had the car independently inspected. The report found several items I would like to discuss." This frames the conversation as professional and collaborative, not adversarial. Sellers respond better to structure than to confrontation.

### Step 2: Lead with the high-severity items

Start with the most expensive finding. "The turbo oil seal is leaking. Three local workshops quoted EUR 1,100 to EUR 1,400 for replacement." This anchors the negotiation at the highest plausible repair cost.

### Step 3: Stack the medium-severity items

Add the brake discs (EUR 340), the cabin filter (EUR 45), and the coolant that tested 8 percent below freeze-point specification (EUR 80 for a flush). The cumulative total matters more than any single item.

### Step 4: Propose a fair adjustment

"The total remediation cost is approximately EUR 1,800 to EUR 2,200. I would like to adjust the price by EUR 1,500 to account for the work I will need to have done." This leaves room for the seller to feel they are meeting you halfway while still capturing the majority of the repair value.

### What buyers get wrong

The most common negotiation mistake is presenting findings as deal-breakers instead of price adjusters. Unless the inspection reveals a safety-critical or catastrophic defect (frame corrosion, engine internal damage, flood damage), most findings are negotiation levers, not walk-away signals.

Our data shows:

- 72 percent of inspected vehicles are worth buying at a reduced price
- 19 percent have issues that make the asking price too high relative to market alternatives
- 9 percent have defects severe enough to recommend walking away

The inspection's value is not just in what it finds — it is in how it reframes your decision from "should I buy this car?" to "what is this car actually worth?"

## The Break-Even Math

For an inspection to pay for itself, it needs to generate savings equal to its cost. At EUR 195, that means identifying a single finding worth at least EUR 195 in repair costs. Given that 62 percent of inspected vehicles have at least one finding over EUR 200, the break-even probability is strong.

But the real calculation includes the scenarios where the inspection prevents a bad purchase entirely:

| Outcome | Probability | Financial impact |
|---|---|---|
| Inspection finds nothing significant | 28% | EUR -195 (inspection fee, no savings) |
| Inspection finds negotiable defects | 53% | EUR +1,145 average net savings |
| Inspection prevents a bad purchase | 19% | EUR +3,200 average loss avoided |

**Expected value per inspection: EUR +1,180** (probability-weighted average across all outcomes).

Even in the worst case — the 28 percent of inspections that find nothing material — the buyer gains confidence that they are paying a fair price. That peace of mind has a value, even if it does not appear on a balance sheet.

## A Checklist for Maximising Inspection ROI

Whether you use Truvis or any other inspection service, these five practices maximise the return on your investment:

- **Inspect before you fall in love** — emotional attachment to a vehicle makes it harder to walk away from bad findings. Inspect early in the decision process, not as a final confirmation step
- **Request a cold-start inspection** — insist that the vehicle is not started before the inspector arrives. Cold-start behaviour reveals turbo, chain, glow plug, and injector issues that disappear once the engine warms up
- **Get the full service history in advance** — send it to the inspector before the appointment so they can cross-reference it against the manufacturer maintenance schedule and identify gaps
- **Ask for remediation cost estimates** — a finding without a price tag has no negotiation value. Every item in the report should include a realistic repair cost range
- **Use the report in writing** — send the key findings to the seller by email or message before the negotiation meeting. This gives them time to process the information and reduces the chance of a defensive reaction in person

> **Inspector's note:** The best inspections are the ones that are boring. A clean report with no major findings means you are buying a solid vehicle at a fair price. Do not see a clean inspection as wasted money — see it as the data you needed to buy with confidence.

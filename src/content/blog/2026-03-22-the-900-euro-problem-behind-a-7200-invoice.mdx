The timing chain tensioner had failed at 89,000 km on a 2016 Audi A4 2.0 TDI, and the buyer did not find out until the engine seized three weeks after purchase. The repair invoice arrived at EUR 7,200, which was EUR 900 more than the purchase price itself. This is the anatomy of a preventable loss, broken down component by component.

We reconstructed this case from the inspection report, the repair shop invoice, and a conversation with the buyer. Every cost figure is real. Every failure point was detectable with the right checklist.

## The Vehicle Profile

The Audi A4 was listed on a popular classifieds platform at EUR 6,300. On paper, it looked like a solid deal:

- **Mileage:** 89,400 km — relatively low for a nine-year-old diesel
- **Service book:** stamped up to 60,000 km at the dealer, then two independent garage visits
- **MOT history:** clean, with no advisories on the last two inspections
- **Exterior and interior:** well-maintained, no visible damage

The buyer drove 140 km to view the car. The engine started cleanly, the test drive felt smooth, and the seller provided a thick folder of receipts. The sale closed the same afternoon at EUR 6,300.

## What the Test Drive Missed

Three critical signals were present during the viewing but went undetected because the buyer did not know what to listen for.

### Signal 1: The 2-Second Cold-Start Rattle

The buyer arrived while the engine was already warm. The seller had "just moved it out of the garage." A warm engine masks the cold-start timing chain rattle that would have been audible within the first two seconds of a genuine cold start.

> **Inspector's note:** Always insist on a stone-cold start. If the engine is warm when you arrive, walk away and come back another day, or ask the seller to leave it overnight. The cold-start window reveals more about chain and tensioner health than any OBD scan.

### Signal 2: A Gap in the Service History

The service book showed dealer stamps until 60,000 km, then two independent stamps at 72,000 km and 85,000 km. What was missing: any record of the timing chain tensioner being checked or the chain slack being measured. On this engine (EA288 2.0 TDI), the manufacturer recommends a tensioner inspection at 80,000 km.

### Signal 3: Faint Oil Weepage at the Timing Cover

A thin film of oil along the timing cover gasket was visible with a torch but easy to dismiss as "normal for the age." In reality, it indicated the cover had been under stress from chain vibration, which loosened the gasket seal.

## The Failure Cascade

Three weeks after purchase, at approximately 90,100 km, the chain jumped two teeth on the intake camshaft sprocket. Here is the sequence, reconstructed from the workshop's teardown report:

1. **Tensioner piston wear** — the hydraulic tensioner had lost 40 percent of its travel due to internal seal degradation. Cost to replace at this stage (if caught): EUR 350 parts + EUR 550 labour = **EUR 900**
2. **Chain slack** — without adequate tension, the chain developed 6 mm of play (manufacturer tolerance: max 2 mm)
3. **Chain skip** — under acceleration entering a motorway on-ramp, the chain jumped teeth
4. **Valve-piston contact** — the intake valves on cylinders 2 and 3 contacted the pistons. Four valves bent immediately
5. **Cylinder head damage** — the valve seats on cylinders 2 and 3 were cracked from the impact

## The Repair Invoice

The workshop provided an itemised breakdown:

- Cylinder head removal and inspection: EUR 480
- New cylinder head (reconditioned): EUR 1,850
- Timing chain kit (chain, tensioner, guides, sprockets): EUR 620
- Valve set (16 valves): EUR 540
- Head gasket set: EUR 180
- Timing cover gasket: EUR 45
- Engine oil and filter: EUR 85
- Coolant flush and refill: EUR 60
- Labour (22 hours at EUR 95/hr): EUR 2,090
- VAT and sundries: EUR 1,250

**Total: EUR 7,200**

The buyer had paid EUR 6,300 for the car. The total cost of ownership after one month: EUR 13,500 for a vehicle with a market value of approximately EUR 7,000 in good running condition.

## What a Pre-Purchase Inspection Would Have Found

A structured 90-minute inspection following the Truvis protocol would have flagged three items:

- **Timing chain tensioner play** — measured via the OBD chain-length adaptation value and confirmed by removing the oil filler cap to listen for chain noise at idle. Estimated detection time: 4 minutes
- **Service history gap** — the missing 80,000 km tensioner check would have been flagged by cross-referencing the service book against the manufacturer maintenance schedule. Estimated detection time: 2 minutes
- **Timing cover oil weepage** — documented with a photograph and rated as "investigate before purchase." Estimated detection time: 1 minute

Total detection time for all three signals: under 10 minutes.

## Lessons for Buyers

This case is not unusual. Timing chain tensioner failures on the EA288 and EA888 engine families are well-documented, and the failure pattern is nearly identical across the VAG group (Audi, VW, Skoda, SEAT).

- **Never accept a warm-start viewing** — the cold-start rattle is your single most valuable diagnostic signal for chain health
- **Cross-reference the service book** against the manufacturer maintenance schedule — missing inspections are as informative as failed ones
- **Budget for the tensioner** on any VAG diesel over 80,000 km — if it has not been done, it needs doing, and that cost belongs in your negotiation
- **Treat oil weepage as a symptom**, not a cosmetic issue — trace it to its source before dismissing it

> **Inspector's note:** The EUR 900 tensioner replacement was the entire preventable cost in this case. Every subsequent line item on the EUR 7,200 invoice was a direct consequence of that single worn component. Prevention is not just cheaper than repair — it is a different order of magnitude.

# Content Voice Review — Truvis Blog

> Reference: NFR40 — 70/30 Inspector/Ally voice ratio

## The 70/30 Voice Ratio

Every Truvis blog article uses a **70/30 Inspector/Ally** voice:

- **70% Inspector** — authoritative, technically grounded, specific. Uses exact figures, named components, named failure modes. Writes with the confidence of someone who has seen the problem hundreds of times.
- **30% Ally** — approachable, encouraging, empathetic. Breaks the inspector tone with conversational asides, practical advice, and reminders that the reader's goal is to buy with confidence, not to become a mechanic.

## What Makes a Good Opening Hook

The first paragraph must be **specific and concrete**. Lead with numbers, named components, or named failure modes.

**Good examples:**

- "The injector pump alone costs EUR 1,400 to replace on a common-rail diesel..."
- "The timing chain tensioner had failed at 89,000 km on a 2016 Audi A4 2.0 TDI..."
- "Three minutes of negotiation saved EUR 2,100 on a 2018 Golf..."

**Bad examples (do not use):**

- "Buying a used car can be stressful..." — generic, passive, no specificity
- "Have you ever wondered about used car inspections?" — rhetorical, unfocused
- "In this article, we will discuss..." — meta-commentary, not content

## Actionable Content Requirement

Every article must include at least one **actionable checklist or specific guidance item** the reader can apply immediately. This can take the form of:

- A bulleted checklist of inspection steps
- A numbered procedure for a specific test
- A cost table the reader can use in negotiation
- A decision framework (when to buy, when to walk away)

## Block Quotes as Inspector's Notes

Use block quotes for the **Inspector's note** voice device — short, direct insights that feel like a senior inspector leaning over and telling you the one thing that matters most:

> **Inspector's note:** A single failed injector on a 2.0 TDI runs EUR 380 for the part alone. Most workshops recommend replacing all four at once.
